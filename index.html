Greenland is Melting

One of Earth's last great ice sheets is disappearing before our eyes.

266 Gt/yr mass loss · 11 mm sea level rise · Arctic +3.3 °C
↓

Ice Mass Loss (GRACE-FO)

Greenland has lost over 4,700 Gt of ice since 2002 — enough to fill Lake Superior twice. Source: Our World in Data / NASA GRACE.

The CO₂ Connection

As CO₂ crossed 400 ppm in 2013, ice loss accelerated sharply. Source: Our World in Data / NOAA.

Surface Melt (MODIS)

2002-07-01 97 % Melt Event!

In July 2012, 97 % of the ice sheet surface showed signs of melting — a once-in-800-year event now recurring.

Surface Elevation Change (CryoSat-2)

−22 cm/yr 0 +3 cm/yr

CryoSat-2 radar altimetry reveals the margins are thinning up to 22 cm per year while the interior gains slight snowfall accumulation — a pattern that accelerates calving at the edges.

Glacier Retreat — Jakobshavn Timelapse

Jakobshavn Glacier has retreated 35 km since 1850 — the pace tripled after 2000. Observe the accelerating retreat in this Google Earth Engine satellite timelapse (1984–2022). Source: Google Earth Engine / Landsat.

Temperature Anomaly (Ilulissat)

Surface temperatures at Ilulissat are now 3.1 °C above the 1981–2010 baseline.

Bedrock Profile (BedMachine v5)

Much of Greenland's bedrock lies below sea level — making it vulnerable to irreversible marine ice sheet instability.
