Greenland is Melting

One of Earth's last great ice sheets is disappearing before our eyes. Greenland is one of the most rapidly changing environments on Earth, its mass loss driven by a complex system of atmospheric warming, ocean heat intrusion, and accelerating feedback mechanisms.

266 Gt/yr mass loss · 11 mm sea level rise · Arctic +3.3 °C

The Scale of the Melt

Satellite gravity measurements reveal a staggering reality: Greenland has shed over 4,700 gigatonnes of ice since 2002. In the last 26 years alone, this melting ice has added 11 millimeters to global sea level rise, threatening hundreds of millions of people in low-lying coastal regions worldwide. Source: Our World in Data / NASA GRACE.

Fueling the Melt: The Rise of CO₂

Human-driven carbon emissions act as the primary catalyst for this unprecedented melting. Rising atmospheric CO₂ levels (now >420 ppm) drive Arctic amplification — a phenomenon where the Arctic warms at two to four times the global average rate. The region has already warmed by 3.3°C compared to pre-industrial levels, with no sign of slowing. Source: Our World in Data / NOAA.

Surface Melt (MODIS)

2002-07-01 97 % Melt Event!

In July 2012, 97% of the ice sheet surface showed signs of melting — an extreme event becoming recurrent. This triggers a dangerous ice-albedo feedback: melting ice reveals darker ocean and land that absorb the sun's energy, further accelerating the melt. Meanwhile, surface meltwater draining through moulins provides basal lubrication, speeding up the ice flow toward the ocean.

Surface Elevation Change (CryoSat-2)

−22 cm/yr 0 +3 cm/yr

CryoSat-2 radar altimetry reveals the margins are thinning up to 22 cm per year. This coastal thinning is largely driven by ocean heat intrusion — warm subpolar ocean water moving into the Arctic basin — which relentlessly undermines marine-terminating glaciers from below.

Glacier Retreat — Jakobshavn Timelapse

Jakobshavn Glacier has retreated 35 km since 1850 — the pace tripled after 2000. It now discharges roughly 46 km³ of ice into the ocean each year. Retreating past critical thresholds risks crossing tipping points into marine ice sheet instability, causing irreversible collapse regardless of future emissions cuts. Source: Google Earth Engine / Landsat.

Temperature Anomaly (Ilulissat)

At the local scale, Inuit communities in Ilulissat face direct cultural, economic, and physical consequences. Destabilised and unpredictable sea-ice seasons severely threaten their traditional hunting practices and coastal infrastructure.
